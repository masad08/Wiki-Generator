Historically, medical decision-making was paternalistic – physicians made choices on behalf of patients, believing they knew what was best, and patients were expected to comply (Kilbride and Joffe, 2018). In the mid-20th century, this began to shift toward patient autonomy, spurred by the rise of informed consent and patients’ rights to accept or refuse treatments(Igel and Lerner, 2016). By the 1980s, shared decision-making (SDM) had gained traction as the preferred model for clinical encounters(Elwyn et al., 2012). SDM is characterized by a collaborative process: clinicians share medical information and options, and patients contribute their values and preferences, with both parties jointly arriving at decisions that align with the patient’s goals. 






This evolution – from physician-driven decisions to a partnership model – marked a fundamental change in the patient–physician relationship, aiming to respect patient autonomy while leveraging physician expertise
